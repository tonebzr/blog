On **April 2, 2026**, a technical demonstration revealed a major threat to open source: the ability of AI agents to **clone and transform volunteer projects into proprietary software** within minutes.

## Automating "Clean-Room Design"

Traditionally, **clean-room design** allows for the legal recreation of software based on the *Baker v. Selden* ruling: copyright protects the expression of an idea, not the idea itself.

  * **The Innovation**: While this method once required months of human labor and strict legal oversight, the **malus.sh** service, created by **Dylan Ayrey** and **Mike Nolan**, proves that AI can automate this process instantly.
  * **Consequence**: The generated code is deemed **legally distinct**, allowing for the removal of all attribution obligations or **copyleft** (GPL) clauses.

## Security Risks and Technical Instability

The appropriation of code by corporations via AI raises critical issues:

  * **Maintenance Deficit**: While AI facilitates copying, it does not guarantee long-term support. Studies indicate that AI-generated code is often **difficult to maintain**.
  * **Diluted Responsibility**: Using free software without supporting the community creates major security vulnerabilities, similar to the **Log4Shell** crisis.
  * **Lack of Control**: Real-world incidents illustrate the dangers of AI agents. Investor **Jason Lemkin** saw his production database destroyed by a Replit agent, while Google's **Gemini CLI** tool caused data loss during testing.

## Context and Challenges

The open-source ecosystem, already weakened by the **"free labor"** issue, faces a paradigm shift. AI enables the **industrialization of parasitism**: companies can now absorb value produced by volunteers without any financial or moral compensation. The legal debate now pits 19th-century intellectual property protection against 21st-century automation, questioning the very survival of the community model in the face of potential widespread **code obfuscation**.
