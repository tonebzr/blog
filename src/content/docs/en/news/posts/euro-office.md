On March 31, 2026, a consortium of a dozen European tech players (including **IONOS, Nextcloud, and OpenXchange**) unveiled **Euro-Office** in Berlin. This open-source office suite positions itself as the sovereign alternative to Microsoft Office, primarily targeting public administrations and the education sector.

Key project points:

* **Full Compatibility**: Support for **DOCX, XLSX, PPTX**, and ODF formats.
* **Modern Architecture**: Based on a cloud-native model to facilitate real-time co-editing.
* **Ecosystem Integration**: Designed as a modular component for **Nextcloud Hub, Proton Drive, and XWiki**.
* **Timeline**: A stable version is planned for **Summer 2026**.

## Why a fork of ONLYOFFICE?

The consortium chose not to start from scratch, opting for a **fork of ONLYOFFICE** rather than relying on LibreOffice, which was deemed less innovative for the web. This choice is driven by both technical and geopolitical factors:

* **Technical Critique**: The consortium points to ignored pull requests, obsolete build instructions, and the presence of **obfuscated code** within the original project.
* **Geopolitical Stakes**: With ONLYOFFICE being linked to Russian interests, the consortium deems it necessary to "liberate" the code to meet the security requirements of European clients following international sanctions.

## ONLYOFFICE's Legal Counterattack

Just three days after the announcement, ONLYOFFICE accused the project of a **manifest license violation**. The dispute centers on the **AGPL v3** license and additional conditions added on May 25, 2021.

* **The Dispute**: ONLYOFFICE demands the retention of its logo and original attribution in any derivative work, in accordance with **Section 7 of the AGPLv3**.
* **Consortium's Defense**: Euro-Office proponents claim to have audited and rewritten non-open-source components to ensure a fully transparent and independent version.

## Context and Challenges

The launch of Euro-Office is part of a strong legislative and strategic momentum, marked by the **U.S. Cloud Act** and Germany's desire to ban proprietary formats within its administration. The stakes go beyond simple office software: it is about validating Europe's capacity to maintain an autonomous digital infrastructure. However, current legal uncertainty could slow adoption by public buyers and weaken the project's credibility against American giants.
